Introduction

In this chapter we will learn about the physical state namely Gaseous state in a precise and ways. Any given substance may exist in solid liquid or gaseous state(phase) depending upon the magnitude of two opposite type of tendencies. Thay are:

  1. Mutual attraction/tendencies(MA): Tendencies to bind molecules together.
  2. Esceping tendencies(ET): Tendency to disperse molecules(get away).

So depending on these tendencies if

  • a)MA >> ET The matter will exist in Solid form
  • b)MA > ET The matter will exist in Liquid form
  • c)MA << ET The matter will exist in Gaseous form

On the basis of condition of pressure and temperature the given substance may exist in all three forms.

for eg: Water at Pressure = 1atm
At T = -10°C exist as Ice(Solid)
At T = 27°C exist as Water(Liquid)
At T = 100°C exist as Vapour(Gas)
Hence gaseous states exist in low pressure and high temperature.

Difference between Solid, Liquid and Gas

Solid:

  1. Molecules are very tightly packed
  2. Inter moleculer distance minimum
  3. Force of attraction is maximum
  4. Kinetic Energy(KE) is minimum
  5. Have definite shape and volume
  6. Density is high
  7. Compressibility and expansibility minimum

Liquid:

  1. Molecules are only tightly packed
  2. Inter moleculer distance is intermediate
  3. iorce of attraction is intermediate
  4. Kinetic Energy(KE) is intermediate
  5. Have definite volume and indefinite shape
  6. Density is intermediate
  7. Compressibility and expansibility is intermediate

Gas:

  1. Molecules are very loosely packed
  2. Inter moleculer distance is maximum
  3. Force of attraction is minimum
  4. Kinetic Energy(KE) is maximum
  5. Have indefinite volume and shape
  6. Density is minimum
  7. Compressibility and expansibility is maximum

Gas Laws

Gas laws are those laws which shows relation between any two of Pressure, Volume and Temperature keeping any third property and amount of gas constant are known as Gas laws.

1) Boyle's law:

For a given amount of gas at constant temperature, pressure of any gas is inversely proportional to its volume.

Mathematical form:
P α 1/V (T = constant, n = constant)
P = k(1/V) (k is a proportionaity conatant and it is depends upon temperature and it is directly proportional to temperature)

P1V1= P2V2 (T, n = constant)
(Trick: All these laws leads to universal gas law which is PV = nRT. In Boyle's law P and V are varied and T is constant so from universal gas law PV = nRT we can say that k may be equivalent to nRT. So k is directly proportional to T.)

2) Charles law:

For a given amount of gas at constant pressure , volume of the gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.
Mathematical form:
V α T (n, P = constant)
V = kT (k is a proportionality constant, from ideal gas equation PV = nRT we get V = (nR/p)T from which we can say that k = nR/P which is inversely proportional to Pressure i.e. k α 1/P)
So,

V/T = Constant
V1/T1 = V2/T2

Graphical Representation of Charles Law

Charles Law alternative form

Charles law also can be atated as, at constant pressure, the volume of a given mass of a gas increases or decreases by 1/273 of its volume at 0°C for each one degree rise or fall in temperature. Which can be represented as:

Vt = V0 + (V0 × t/273)
Where,
Vt = Volume at any temperature at t°C;
V0 = Volume at 0°C;
1/273 = Temperature co-efficient of Volume;

Question: At what temperature Vt = 0
Solution: We have Vt = V0 + (V0 × t/273)
If t = -273°C then Vt = 0
So t = -273°C = 0 Kelvin; It is also called absolute zero temperature i.e. Temperature at which volume of gas became zero. It is the lowest possible temperature. Thats why we use Kelvin scale of temperature as SI unit in order to avoid negative temperature in Degree celcius scale.

Ideal Gas Equation(IGE)

1) PV/T = R (Experimentally, for 1 mole of gas) For 1 mole of gas value of PV/T at different condition(Different value of P,V and T) is always constant. Its value is denoted by R known as Universal Gas Constant(PV = RT)(IGE) for 1 mole.

2) PV/T = nR (For n mole)   ⇒ PV = nRT

Significance of Universal Gas Constant(R):
We have PV = nRT    ⇒ R = PV/nT
In mks system, P = N/m2, V = m3, T = K and n = mole, putting all these values in above expression we get R = (Nm)/(mol K);
but Nm = Joule, Hence R = Workdone/mole.Kelvin
So we can state R as the Workdone on a gas per unit mole and per unit Kelvin.

Values of R:
R is universal gas constant so its value can be calculated for any condition of pressure , temperature will give the same value. But for simplicity R's value is always calculated at STP for 1 mole of gas.

Due to different values of P and V in different system R has different values and units. For calculating values of Universal Gas Constant we will take n = 1 mole and Temperature and Pressure be at STP.

Different values of R:

  1. Litre-atm/mole-Kelvin:
    Let P = 1 atm, V = 22.4L and T = 273K
    R = (1 × 22.4)/(1 × 273) = 0.0821 Lit-atm/mole-K
    For simplicity in calculation and to reduce time in exam you can use R = 0.08 or 1/12.
  2. Joule/mole-Kelvin:
    Let P = 101.325×103 N/m2, V = 22.4 × 10-3 m3 and T = 273K
    R = (101.325×103 × 22.4× 10-3)/(1 × 273) = 8.314 J/mole-K;    This unit is generally used in energy and velocity related problems.

Ideal Gas Equation in Terms of Density

Form Ideal Gas Equation we have PV = nRT
Now mole can be written as n = Given mass/Molar mass = W/M
⇒ PV = (W/M)RT
⇒ P = W/V × (RT/M) [now Density, d = W/V]
⇒ P = (dRT/M)     ⇒ d = PM/RT
Above formula is for calculating density of single gas only, however if mixture of gases are present then we have to take average of Molar mass of all the gases then above formula can be written as d = (PMav)/RT where Mav = Total mass of gas/total moles of gas. The unit of density is gram/Litre.

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressure

Partial Pressure: Pressure exerted by an individual gas present in the same containers at same temperature is known as partial pressure of gas.

According to this law total pressure exerted by a mixture of non-reacting gases in a container of volume V at temperature T is equal to summation of partial pressure of component gases present in the mixture.
Ptotal = P1 + P2 + P3 + .....+ Pn
Where,
       Ptotal = Total pressure
       P1 = Partial pressure of Gas-1
       P2 = Partial Pressure of Gas-2

Calculation of Partial Pressure of gas:


IMAGE
Let the container having volume 'V' contains two gases Gas-1 and Gas-2. P1 and P2 be the partial pressure and PT be the total pressure exerted by Gas-1 and Gas-2. n1 and n2 be the total moles Gas-1 and Gas-2.
Now, using Ideal Gas Equation PT × V = (n1 + n2)RT [equation (i)]
For Gas-1: P1V = n1RT [equation (ii)]
For Gas-2: P2V = n2RT [equation (iii)]
Adding equation (ii) and equation (iii) we get (P1 + P2)V = (n1 + n2)RT [equation (iv)]
On comparing equation (iv) and equation (i) we get PTV = (P1 + P2)V
⇒ PTV = (P1 + P2)V

Kinetic Theory of Gases

Main Postulates

  1. Gas made up of large number of small tiny particles known as molecules
  2. Molecules of a single gas are identical in size, shape as well as mass but different from molecules of another gas.
  3. Effect of force of gravity on gas molecules is negligible due to their very small mass.
  4. Gas molecules are not at rest but posses rapid random motion in straight line in all direction.
  5. During their motion molecules of gas collide with wall of the container and this collision are considered to be perfectly elastic(Energy and momentum is conserved).
  6. Pressure developed by the gas is due to this collisions
  7. Average KE of a gas sample depends only upon absolute temperature
  8. Volume occupied by gas molecules is negligible in comparision to the volume of container
  9. There is no force of attraction between gas molecules

All these postulates are valid only for ideal gases because for real gases last two postulates are not valid

Maxwells Distribution of Molecular Speed

  1. It is based upon law of probability.
  2. It gives statistical average of speeds of molecules of whole collection of gas.
  3. Graph between fraction of molecules and speed is a bell shaped graph.
  4. Molecules corresponding to zero speed is very less as well as fraction of molecules corresponding to very high speed is also very less.
  5. Maximum fraction of molecules posses a common velocity which is represented by the peak of the curve and it is known as Most Probable Speed.
  6. Area under curve is constant at all temperature and it equals to unity.
  7. On increasing the temperature curve becames flatter i.e. it shift to the right hand side but peak comes downward.

Real Gas(Non Ideal Gas)

Difference between real and ideal gas

Ideal gas: A gas which follows ideal gas equation at all pressure and temperature is known as ideal gas. For ideal gas volume occupied by gas molecule is negligible as well as force of attraction between gas molecules is also negligible.

Real gas: A gas which follows ideal gas equation at low pressure and high temperature is known as real gas. Real gas molecule has sufficient volume as well as significant force of attraction between them.
